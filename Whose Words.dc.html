A glimpse, not a simulation

Whose words?

This prototype is an evocation of one repeated choice a person makes when they speak through a brain-computer interface. The choice is, “Do I accept this version of what I wanted to say or not?” Using the exercise below I am attempting to make the trade-off between communication clarity and "good-enough" feel less abstract to the average able-bodied person. The person using such a system should always feel like the author. The AI should always remain a tool.

What is real · what isn’t

The interaction above is a Wizard of Oz prototype, a common design method where the ‘smart’ system is faked so you can test the experience before building the real thing. The responses are scripted. The energy meter and the word predictions represent a real struggle: the more an AI fills in what you’re trying to say, the faster you can “speak,” but the harder it can be to keep the words truly yours. That slow loosening of authorship is what is called “drift.” The trade-off you just felt is real: studies have found people giving up some control of their exact words for speed, and AI assistance quietly changing what a person meant. The energy meter and the fatigue framing help you empathize with the people using these systems.

Deeper analysisThe written explanation behind this pieceThe human-AI relationship it explores, and what that means for design.

The human-AI relationship being analyzed

Whose Words? focuses on the relationship between an individual who has lost their ability to verbally communicate and a speech brain-computer interface that attempts to anticipate and complete speech for the individual. The individual bypasses the system to gain an external vocal apparatus. However, each predicted word is also an invisible assertion about the speaker’s identity. The work examines a recurring question. At what point does your speech get lost when saying it requires more of the machine to do the talking?

How the artifact communicates that analysis

Rather than attempting to articulate the effects of that trade-off to you, the prototype provides a more direct understanding. You become the speaker, and the prototype displays the remaining capacity to speak using the construct of an energy meter that is drained each time you attempt to speak. In each turn, you are presented with the same challenge as the people who use the brain-computer interface. You can accept the phrasing provided by the machine, which is quick, or expend the little energy you have to replace the phrasing with something less machine-like. Because the cost of fixing the phrasing is always greater than the cost of accepting the phrasing, you lose energy to the machine’s version rather than to gain anything from it.

How it connects to course concepts and evidence

The “accept or fix” challenge is an example of the Judge–Advisor System (Sniezek & Buckley, 1995) in which you have an intention, receive advice, and then make a decision. In this situation, rather than relying on a system’s accuracy, Sniezek and Van Swol (2001) show that trust exists with other variables. The authorship stakes come from the design work of speech-neuroprosthesis that places user control and “speech ownership” at the forefront (Sankaran et al., 2023; Freudenburg et al., 2024). The drift that it visualizes corresponds to the fact that AI assistance can gradually alter what a person intended to say.

Design insights and implications

The most important design insight is that user agency decreases gradually. It does not disappear suddenly. Agency erosion can be the result of many incremental design decisions that appear to be beneficial from a design perspective (e.g., decisions that result in the system behaving in a more efficient manner). Therefore, there is value in “measuring” agency as a design criterion. To improve user agency, make the corrections affordable, and show users when a suggestion is deviating from what the user meant. Focus on “whose words these are” because it is a design goal rather than an afterthought.

Sources are the studies cited above: Sniezek & Buckley (1995); Sniezek & Van Swol (2001); Sankaran et al. (2023); Freudenburg et al. (2024).
