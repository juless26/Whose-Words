A glimpse, not a simulation

Whose words?

This prototype is an evocation of one repeated choice a person makes when they speak through a brain-computer interface. The choice is, “Do I accept this version of what I wanted to say or not?” Using the exercise below I am attempting to make the trade-off between communication clarity and "good-enough" feel less abstract to the average able-bodied person. The person using such a system should always feel like the author. The AI should always remain a tool.

The setting

You are at dinner with your daughter. She has just been offered a job three thousand miles away, and she asked what you think. You know exactly what you want to say. Saying it is the work.

How this works
  1. 01You decode your reply one word at a time. It takes effort, and you will watch the seconds pass.
  2. 02Partway through, the system offers to finish your sentence for you, fast, but not quite in your words.
  3. 03Correcting it back to your words costs energy. You only have so much. Spend it where it matters.

What is real · what isn’t

The interaction above is a Wizard of Oz prototype, a common design method where the ‘smart’ system is faked so you can test the experience before building the real thing. The responses are scripted. The energy meter and the word predictions represent a real struggle: the more an AI fills in what you’re trying to say, the faster you can “speak,” but the harder it can be to keep the words truly yours. That slow loosening of authorship is what is called drift. The trade-off you just felt is real: studies have found people giving up some control of their exact words for speed, and AI assistance quietly changing what a person meant. The energy meter and the fatigue framing help you empathize with the people using these systems.

A · THE SUBJECT

Below are some studies about this topic. 

BRAINGATE · CONSORTIUM

A long-running research collaboration testing implanted intracortical microelectrode (“Utah”) arrays for people with paralysis, across several institutions over roughly two decades. 

STANFORD / BRAINGATE · NATURE 2023

An intracortical speech BCI decoded the attempted speech of a participant with ALS at 62 words per minute, with a 23.8% word-error rate on a 125,000-word vocabulary. The authors note this “begins to approach the speed of natural conversation (160 words per minute).”

Willett et al., Nature 620, 1031-1036 (2023)
UCSF / UC BERKELEY · NATURE 2023

Using high-density recordings from the cortical surface, researchers decoded text at a median 78 words per minute (25% median word-error rate) for a participant paralyzed by a brainstem stroke. Her existing communication device ran at about 14 wpm.

Metzger et al., Nature 620, 1037-1046 (2023)
UC DAVIS / BRAINGATE · 2024-2025

For a participant with ALS (Casey Harrell), a 256-electrode system reached about 97-99% word accuracy and was used in self-paced conversation (reported around 32-56 wpm) for hundreds of hours.

Card et al., NEJM (2024); UC Davis Health
NOTES ON THE STUDIES ABOVE
  • Each result above involves a single participant in an investigational trial, not an approved product. Whether it generalizes is not yet known.
  • Speeds vary by study, vocabulary size, and whether measured in controlled tests or free conversation. Numbers are ranges, not constants. Casual speech is roughly 150-160 wpm.
  • These systems decode attempted speech, which are signals from the motor cortex as a person tries to speak. The system cannot “mind read.”
B · How this interaction is modelled

Below are studies that influenced the design of this prototype. The studies are about the decision-science behind the "accept-or-correct" mechanism. These are references that help describe how the prototype works.

The accept-or-correct mechanic above is structured as a Judge-Advisor System (JAS): one person (the “judge”) forms a response, receives advice, then commits to a final answer. It is the standard experimental paradigm for studying advice-taking. Established by Sniezek & Buckley in 1995 and, through a 2022 meta-analysis, still the most commonly applied measure of advice-taking in the field for thirty years.

A later study by the same authors found that an ongoing relationship can inflate trust independent of an advisor’s actual accuracy. Across two experiments, one with strangers and one with people in ongoing relationships, judges gave higher and more variable trust ratings of their partner than advisors did, suggesting judges were more motivated to evaluate trust. It is listed here as related context, not a force this prototype reproduces. What actually drives the choice above is: energy. You accept an attempt because correcting it costs more energy than you have left, not because familiarity has made you "trust it."

Sniezek & Buckley, Org. Behavior & Human Decision Processes 62(2), 159-174 (1995)
Sniezek & Van Swol, Org. Behavior & Human Decision Processes 84(2), 288-307 (2001)
C · Whose words, in real research

A question central to this prototype is "who owns the words when a machine helps produce them?" It's a concern speech-BCI researchers are actively raising.

A 2024 editorial in Frontiers in Human Neuroscience frames the open question directly: how to preserve user agency and "speech ownership" as assistive communication devices are driven by neural signals. It sets the agenda; it does not claim to have settled it.

Freudenburg, Berezutskaya & Herbert, Front. Hum. Neurosci. 18 (2024)
